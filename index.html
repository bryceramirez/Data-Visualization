HIV stands for human immunodeficiency virus.

HIV is considered to be one of the world's most serious public health and social problems.

This could reduce the number of CD4 cells which makes the body unable to fight off infections and diseases such as cancer.

AIDS is the most severe type of HIV infection which happens after the final stage of HIV.

Compared to other countries which has dominant HIV cases as shown in the graph, Philippines' cases are increasing while other countries, including South Africa which has the highest number of cases, have decreasing cases. This is why HIV in the Philippines is a topic that we should address and talk about.

As of September 2019, there are a total of 65,463 HIV cases in the Philippines. 5% of these patients have died.

In 2018, the Philippines has been considered as the country with the fastest growing HIV epidemic in Asia and the Pacific by the United Nations Program. However, people still remain silent or are too shy to talk about it.

There are a lot more Jose Maries with stories all over the country as the number of cases increase, and it seems like they don’t exist in everyone’s eyes. Their stories remain untold.

The number of these stories increase every month as the years progress.

The lowest was from April 2017 with 629 cases or stories while the highest was in January 2019 with 1249. From these stories of HIV cases, a number of these would translate to AIDS cases. HIV cannot be cured, there is no vaccine for HIV yet. It has not been below the lowest month since 2016, we can't really say that we are solving the problem. These stories will keep on increasing if we remain indifferent towards it.

These stories of HIV cases at times translate into AIDS cases.

These also increases through the years of 2016 to 2019. March 2019 was the highest having 215 cases. Lowest was from December 2016 with 72 cases. Through early HIV treatment, one can prevent it from progressing to an AIDS case which is far more dangerous.

All ages can be affected by the virus.

Most of these occur at adulthood ranging from 25-34 years old having an average of 51.05% across all results, followed by cases from teenagers at ages around 15 to 24. These cases from adolescents and adults are due to inadequate sex education, substance use, and peer pressure. Older stories follow this such as the range of 35 to 49 and 50 years of age and above. The least stories reported were from children that aged below 15 years old. For the infant cases, on the other hand, most get the virus from an HIV-positive mother to the child. This could occur during pregnancy, childbirth or when breastfeeding.

There are more stories that are heard from males.

Male cases are significantly higher compared to those that are female. The highest cases from each sex were from 2018. Males had 10,862 cases while female cases were at 599. Sexual transmission remains to be the prevalent cause of spreading HIV. With this, it often occurs with homosexual and bisexual men, therefore accounting for a large population of the cases. Anal sex without using condoms or contraceptives are the prevalent and riskiest reason for this amount of male cases.

Stories are heard from different areas in the country.

Most of these cases occur at NCR having the highest amount of cases. This is followed by regions 4A, 7, 3 and 11 with significant number of cases in recent years. These facts remain true as NCR is considered as the most densely populated region with a population density of 20,785 persons per square kilometer. In line with this, regions 4A and 7 are also densely populated which attribute to their signifcant number of cases. The other regions that were not presented have been generalized by the Department of Health as ROTC (rest of the county), due to their minimal amount of cases. Altogether, the cases from the rest of the country would amount to 9936. There might be more undiagnosed or unaccounted cases.

At times, their stories conclude to a tragic end.

The deaths caused by HIV also increases each year as presented. The highest amount of which occurred in April 2019 which recorded a total of 189 deaths.

Stories or HIV cases are told in these testing centers.

These contain the necessary equipment to diagnose such disease. With this, most of the testing centers belong to Luzon. Among all the regions, NCR, contains the highest number of testing center hospitals with an amount of 122. Least of which is region 2 with 2 testing centers. Get tested, get diagnosed early.

With these concentration of stories and distribution centers together,

It is evident that the location of these hospitals correspond towards the concentration of the cases or stories that occur. As most of the cases are from the NCR region, this is also true for the number of testing centers. Treatment is a rigorous process which requires constant checkups and maintenance.

Though these stories are told, they aren’t really heard by everyone else. These are not understood appropriately by everyone. Most of us think about them as tales of horror causing us to fear them. We build a wall around them, creating stigmas and promoting discrmination in workplaces, learning institutions, hospitals, and in public.

But these are not stories of horror and terror, these are tales of tragedy; a cry for help.

We fear what we do not understand. There are a lot of misconceptions about HIV that greatly hinders the treatment for various cases. As we fear them, they fear themselves too. A lot of Jose Maries become afraid of getting tested due to the fear of being shunned away.

Instead, the right way is for us to be educated, and reach out to these stories. The conservative tradition of Filipinos towards HIV makes matters worse. The silence and unsympathetic stance for these stories create more harm for victims. What Jose Marie needs is acceptance, access to knowledge, and proper treatment.
